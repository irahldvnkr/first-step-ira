A personal observation
I've noticed something at my economics college: most women I know understand investing well enough to ask the right questions, and most of them haven't started yet. Women make up only 1 in 4 individual mutual fund investors in India (AMFI, 2024). From what I've seen, the gap isn't knowledge. It's a quiet intimidation that turns into inaction. The industry rarely addresses it directly, and platforms that rely on push notifications and content can actually amplify the anxiety rather than reduce it.

You've said you want to build long-term wealth, but you can only leave money invested for under a year. These two things work against each other.
Long-term investing strategies depend on time to recover from short-term volatility. The Nifty 50 fell 38% in early 2020 and recovered fully within 6 months, but only for investors who stayed in. Behind the build
Product thinking
by Ira Haldavanekar · Full write-up on Substack
01 · The problem this is solving
Temporal asymmetry, not information deficit
SEBI's 2025 Investor Survey found that 94% of non-investors are affected by risk and return uncertainty, and 95% struggle with product complexity and information gaps. But 63% of households are already aware of securities products. The information is reaching people. First Step is built around a different insight: the barrier is the emotional distance between understanding investing and doing it with real money, not the absence of knowledge.
02 · The decision mechanics
Why buy/hold/step out, and why holding is surfaced explicitly
The original version used stay/withdraw only. Adding buy and hold makes the simulation more behaviourally honest, real investors have three options, not two. Hold is explicitly noted as a valid, active decision because first-time investors often feel that doing nothing is wrong. It isn't. Surfacing that message at the moment of decision is where the real learning happens.
03 · What we decided not to build
Leaderboards. Social sharing. A bull market period.
Any competitive element shifts the frame from personal learning to performance comparison. Social sharing of returns invites judgment. A period where everything goes up teaches nothing real. Volatility and asset divergence are what build genuine investing intuition.
04 · How to evaluate if this worked
One primary metric
Do users who complete First Step convert to a first real investment at a higher rate than users who go through Groww's existing onboarding? If completion is high but conversion is flat, the bridge to real investment is broken. If completion itself is low, the simulation isn't compelling enough, fix that first.
05 · Negative returns without demotivation
Smaller number. Contextualised explanation. Not a recommendation.
Negative returns are shown smaller and in muted grey, not red, so the number doesn't dominate. A reframe card then appears explaining what that result actually means in the context of the user's goal and the specific historical period. This is not advice. It is retrospective pattern explanation: historical facts about what happened in that market period and why a negative result in that context means something different from what it feels like.
What I'm genuinely uncertain about
Whether 5 snapshots is the right number. Whether Rs. 500 is the right CTA anchor, it's a hypothesis, only user research will find the real number. And whether the Nervous Restarter, someone who invested once, lost money, and stopped, responds differently to a down-market simulation than a first-timer. They may need their own variant with an explicit recovery arc.
